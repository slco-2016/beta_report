Initial Learnings

From our research we learned that while some people are indeed simply forgetting their appointments, there are other social, emotional, financial, logistical, and institutional barriers that account for people failing to appear at court and complying with their supervision.


"Clients that don't have a lot of arrests prior to me seeing them and one that have a good support system are the ones more likely to be successful."
- Criminal Justice Services Case Manager


"Every client comes a different kind of need. You treat each one is a separate way ... some that need more encouragement some that need more guidance."
- Criminal Justice Services Case Manager


"[Texting] would be good for my clients who are homeless or are financially strapped and only use phones for texting."
- Criminal Justice Services Case Manager


Revising the Problem Statement

Supervision Clients are juggling a lot - like finding and keeping a job, staying sober, taking care of their kids, and keeping a roof over their heads. Maintaining consistent, open communication with their case managers is key to navigating all of this while completing supervision successfully.

Current Problem Statement

We revised our problem statement taking into consideration what we learned from people struggling with supervision appointments and how they thought about this problem: Supervision Clients in Salt Lake County and their case managers need to communicate more easily so clients understand everything they need to do and case managers can help remove obstacles to can complete supervision successfully.
